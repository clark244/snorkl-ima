01

About This Report

This report has two purposes: first, to articulate a working model of the mechanisms through which Snorkl produces impact — from product through teacher implementation and student use to intended student outcomes; and second, to use that model to identify and prioritize opportunities for impact measurement. The model is the analytical foundation. The measurement priorities follow directly from it.

The analysis draws on three sources: a discovery conversation with Snorkl co-founder Jon Laven on June 1, 2026; Snorkl's pre-conversation questionnaire response; and publicly available information at snorkl.app. It is a first-pass working draft, not a final assessment. We expect it to sharpen as Snorkl shares more about its internal data, district relationships, and strategic priorities.

Our aim is not to evaluate Snorkl's impact, but to help Snorkl's leadership see clearly where the causal chain is well understood, where it is assumed but untested, and where the most useful measurement investments should go — in what order, and why. Throughout, we favor measurement that can be built into how the company already operates, rather than externally imposed research projects that consume scarce startup time.

03

Impact Measurement Opportunities

A central finding of this analysis is that Snorkl's measurement opportunity is unusually well-defined. The constructs that matter — how the tool is used, and whether better use produces better outcomes — are all measurable with existing methods. There is no new instrument to invent. The challenge is to instrument and connect what Snorkl already has, and to do so in a way that serves the business without consuming the time and energy that external research partnerships have.

This is reinforced by Snorkl's buyer reality. Today the primary buyer is the tech director, for whom usage alone is often sufficient — a low evidence bar. But academic buyers, and larger districts running formal procurement, increasingly want evidence of impact on state tests, interim assessments, and ELL scores. Snorkl does not yet need to win the hardest evidentiary argument; it needs to be ready for it as the buyer mix shifts. The priorities below are sequenced to build that readiness without over-investing ahead of demand.

We organize the opportunities as a single sequenced track of four priorities. Each is more powerful once the prior one is in place, but the first three can all begin within the next 6–12 months using data Snorkl largely already holds. The fourth is a longer-horizon differentiator.

The Four Priorities

The priorities are sequenced: each is more meaningful if the prior one is in place. Priorities 1 through 3 draw largely on data Snorkl already holds and can begin in the near term; Priority 4 is a longer-horizon differentiator.

1 Measure Implementation Quality — The Behavioral Foundation Now Start Here ▼

This is the right place to start because it requires no new data collection — only turning data Snorkl already captures into an analytic signal. Jon's single most-wanted answer is whether strong implementation produces better outcomes than weak or no implementation. That question is unanswerable until implementation quality is defined as a measurable variable rather than something observed anecdotally on classroom visits. The backend already knows whether a student used voice or whiteboard-only, how many attempts they made, and whether they revised — Snorkl reports an average of 2+ attempts per student. These are the operational signatures of the high-fidelity use Jon describes. A caution: in-platform mastery scores are not a clean outcome measure — a student who attempts an activity five times scores higher than one who attempts it once, reflecting time on task, not necessarily more learning. Keep implementation metrics (how the tool is used) separate from outcomes (Priority 2).

What to measure

  • Voice vs. whiteboard-only use, by classroom / school / district
  • Revision behavior — share of activities with 2+ attempts; attempts per activity
  • Dosage — frequency and consistency of use over the year
  • Teacher-side engagement — use of the insights dashboard; Snorkl Coach availability

This data is almost entirely internal and available now. The opportunity is to treat it as impact-relevant implementation data, not just usage volume.

2 Link Implementation to Student Outcomes — The Core Evidence 6–12 mo The Central Study ▼

This produces evidence larger academic buyers increasingly require. It connects the implementation-quality index from Priority 1 to external achievement data, asking whether classrooms that use Snorkl well show stronger growth than those that use it weakly or not at all.

Student outcomes cannot be measured from Snorkl's internal data alone. The tractable path is the model used by Paloma (another Reach portfolio company): include de-identified data sharing in the terms of service, obtain district benchmark data (i-Ready, STAR, MAP, or state interim/WIDA scores) for fall and spring, and analyze growth internally. Houston ISD's own data pull is a likely prototype — obtaining that report is a concrete first step. Methodologically this is an ESSA Level III (correlational) design: credible, business-aligned, and directly actionable, and it can be strengthened where a district rolls out to some schools before others.

What to measure

  • District-provided benchmark or interim assessment scores, fall and spring (de-identified)
  • Growth associated with each implementation-quality dimension from Priority 1
  • ELL / WIDA outcomes for districts using Snorkl specifically for language development
  • Weak-vs-strong or phased-rollout comparisons to approximate a counterfactual where possible

Start with one or two willing districts (Houston, Des Moines, Twin Rivers) and, ideally, secure data retroactively for the past year so the analysis need not wait for a full cycle.

3 The PD → Implementation Link — What Moves Fidelity 6–12 mo Informs the PD Decision ▼

Jon raised an open strategic question: how much should Snorkl invest in wraparound professional development, and should it be built internally or partnered out? Strategically collected data can inform that decision. With the implementation-quality index from Priority 1 in hand, Snorkl can test whether districts that received PD show stronger implementation than those that did not. In addition, understanding what dimensions of implementation are most strongly associated with outcomes (also Priority 2) can guide the focus of PD to help educators focus on the "active ingredients" of Snorkl.

What to measure

  • Implementation-quality index for districts that received PD vs. those that did not
  • Dose of PD (number/length of calls) against implementation strength
  • Which specific practices (voice, revision, discussion) PD most reliably shifts
  • What to focus on in PD (from Priority 2 findings)

If PD reliably raises implementation quality — and Priority 2 shows implementation quality drives outcomes — Snorkl has a defensible case for investing in (and pricing) PD as an impact lever.

4 Differentiated Measures — Oral Language & Metacognition 12–24 mo High Value, Longer Horizon ▼

Snorkl sits on an asset almost no other tool has: a longitudinal archive of students explaining their reasoning aloud. That verbal data is a direct window into two things that are hard to measure elsewhere — oral-language development (especially for English language learners) and metacognitive skill. Districts are already using Snorkl specifically to track ELL oral-language proficiency over time. Developing a measure that converts recorded explanations into an oral-language or metacognition signal would be genuinely differentiated — the one place Snorkl could contribute something new to the field. It is sequenced last because it depends on the foundational work above and requires specialist measurement partnership.

What to measure

  • Confirm whether students' verbal responses are archived and retrievable for analysis
  • Partner with a measurement / linguistics specialist to define a scorable construct
  • Pilot an oral-language-growth metric with districts already using Snorkl for ELL
  • Explore a metacognition signal derived from explanation transcripts over time

Cobalt Collective can help scope this and connect Snorkl to the right measurement expertise. It is a differentiator worth pursuing once Priorities 1–3 are underway.

04

Recommended Next Steps

The structure above implies a specific set of near-term actions. The first four are low-lift and use data Snorkl largely already holds; the last two set up the longer-horizon work.

1
Implementation-quality dashboard scoping. Define an implementation-quality index from existing backend data — voice vs. whiteboard-only, revision/attempt counts, dosage, dashboard engagement — and scope an admin-level view at the school and district level. A focused working session with the product/data team would be sufficient to start.
2
Get the Houston ISD report. Obtain the data Houston pulled relating Snorkl usage to student outcomes. Whether rigorous or self-serving, it is a prototype for the Priority 2 study and a fast way to learn what district data is reachable.
3
PD-effect analysis. Cross-reference which districts received PD with their implementation-quality index to test whether PD moves fidelity. Low-cost, internal, and directly informs the build-vs-partner PD decision.
4
Data-sharing terms exploration. Assess what it would take — operationally and contractually — to include de-identified benchmark-data sharing in district terms of service, modeled on Paloma. Start with one or two willing districts (Houston, Des Moines, Twin Rivers) rather than a blanket change.
5
Priority 2 study design. With one district's benchmark data in hand (ideally retroactive for the past year), design the implementation-to-outcome analysis as an ESSA Level III correlational study, with opportunistic phased-rollout comparisons where available.
6
Differentiated-measure scoping. Confirm whether students' verbal responses are archived, and scope a partnership to convert them into an oral-language or metacognition measure — the longer-horizon differentiator. Cobalt Collective can help identify the right measurement expertise.
